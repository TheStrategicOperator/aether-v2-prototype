You should form a Single-Member LLC

This gives you liability protection without unnecessary complexity at your current stage.

This is the most efficient structure for where you are right now — not forever, just right now.

Your Situation

You're starting as a solo founder, not raising outside capital, and your expected revenue is still in the early stage. You want to keep things simple while getting protected.

Why this fits you

  • You are the only owner, so this keeps things simple
  • You are not raising investors, so a corporation is unnecessary
  • Your current revenue does not justify S-Corp complexity yet
  • You prioritized simplicity and control

How you're taxed

Your business income flows directly to your personal tax return. You’ll pay self-employment tax (about 15.3%) on your profit.

When to upgrade

If your net profit grows past around $80K, you can consider switching to an S-Corp to reduce taxes. Until then, keeping things simple is the better move.

What not to do

  • S-Corp — adds payroll complexity too early
  • C-Corp — only makes sense if you plan to raise investors
  • Multi-member LLC — not needed as a solo founder

What to do next

  • Choose your formation state
  • File your LLC
  • Get your EIN (free from IRS)
  • Open a business bank account
  • Set up basic bookkeeping
